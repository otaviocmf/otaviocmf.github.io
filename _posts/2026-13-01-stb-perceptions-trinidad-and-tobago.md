## üåä **Introduction: A Small Island State's Vision for Global Agricultural Sustainability**

In a world confronting existential challenges in food security, climate resilience, and social equity, the quest for genuinely sustainable agricultural models has evolved from a technical concern to a civilizational imperative. It is within this context of urgency and hope that the Science and Technology Backyard (STB) concept emerges‚Äîan innovation philosophy that is redefining rural extension paradigms and technology transfer on a global scale.

The International Science and Technology Backyard Forum, held in January 2026, represented far more than an academic gathering‚Äîit constituted a historical inflection point where tradition and innovation, local knowledge and global science, small-scale farmers and leading researchers engaged in profound, transformative dialogue. Over three intensive days, representatives from over forty nations shared not merely data and statistics, but narratives of change that are reshaping rural landscapes across all continents. It was a pleasure for me to work in the conference as a translator for my english speaking colleagues and to gain knowledge and insights about modern agriculture.

### **The Caribbean Context: Unique Challenges, Unique Opportunities**

As a representative from Trinidad and Tobago‚Äîa twin-island state in the Caribbean‚Äîmy participation carried particular significance. Our agricultural reality is characterized by:

1. **Limited land availability** (only 25% of total land area suitable for agriculture)
2. **High vulnerability to climate change** (hurricanes, drought, sea-level rise)
3. **Dependence on food imports** (over 80% of food consumed is imported)
4. **Rich biodiversity** with untapped potential for sustainable cultivation
5. **Strong traditional knowledge** in tropical crop management

The STB model, originally conceived in China, is based on a radically simple yet profoundly powerful premise: instead of bringing farmers to university laboratories, researchers and students move into the backyards‚Äîliterally and metaphorically‚Äîof rural communities. This geographical inversion symbolizes an epistemological revolution: knowledge ceases to flow vertically, top-down, and instead circulates horizontally within collaborative networks where traditional wisdom and scientific understanding mutually enrich each other.

What we witnessed at this forum was the maturation of this concept into complete innovation ecosystems. These are no longer isolated projects or piecemeal initiatives, but integrated systems where agricultural policy, university research, technological development, and community capacity-building converge to create contextualized, scalable solutions. Every session, every panel, every conversation in the corridors revealed a complex mosaic of approaches that, in their diversity, shared a common denominator: an uncompromising commitment to socio-environmental justice and food sovereignty.

##  **A Transformative Experience**

It was a profound honor to participate in the **International Science and Technology Backyard Forum**, an enriching experience that brought together brilliant minds from around the world to discuss sustainable agricultural innovation.

I discovered numerous low-cost technologies that could revolutionize smallholder farming in Caribbean island nations, particularly those addressing:

#### 3. **Global Collaboration Networks**
The event facilitated the creation of an international network of researchers and professionals committed to sustainability, with special attention to Small Island Developing States (SIDS).

## üå± **Technical Analyses and International Cooperation**

### üöú **Development of Machinery for Small Producers in Brazil**

Brazil presented remarkable advances in developing technologies adapted to family farming. During technical sessions, several innovations were demonstrated:

**Highlighted Innovations:**
1. **Low-Power, Affordable Tractors**
   - Specifically designed for small properties
   - Reduced fuel consumption (up to 40% less than conventional machines)
   - Simplified maintenance that farmers can perform themselves

2. **Multifunctional Implements**
   - A single platform adaptable for planting, cultivation, and harvesting
   - Materials developed through university-private sector partnerships (Embrapa + private companies)
   - Cost 60% lower than imported equipment

3. **Accessible Precision Technologies**
   - Low-cost soil sensors developed by Brazilian startups
   - Mobile applications for farm management with simplified interfaces
   - Agricultural drones produced locally with national components

**Impact Observed:**
- Average productivity increase: **35-50%** in pilot properties
- Post-harvest loss reduction: **up to 30%**
- Return on investment: **18-24 months** for farmers
- Job creation in local manufacturing and maintenance

**Relevance to Caribbean Context:** These Brazilian innovations offer valuable lessons for developing appropriate machinery for Caribbean smallholders, particularly the emphasis on simplicity, affordability, and local maintenance capacity.

### üá®üá≥ **Appropriate Fertilizer Use in China: Efficiency Models**

The Chinese delegation shared transformative experiences in nutrient management:

**Integrated Recommendation System:**
- **National digital platform** integrating:
  - Real-time soil analysis
  - Weather forecasting
  - Regional productivity history
  - Grain market prices

**Precision Application Technologies:**
1. **Variable Rate Technology (VRT)**
   - Productivity maps generate specific prescriptions per square meter
   - Average reduction in fertilizer use: **20-35%**
   - Increase in nitrogen use efficiency: **from 30% to 55%**

2. **Controlled-Release Fertilizers**
   - Developed through university-chemical industry partnerships
   - Synchronized release with critical plant phases
   - Leaching reduction: **up to 70% less nitrate in groundwater**

**Results Achieved:**
- **2025-2030 Target**: Reduce chemical fertilizer use by 5 million tons
- **Efficiency increase**: From 35% to 42% in N-P-K utilization
- **Training programs**: 2 million farmers trained in integrated management

**Caribbean Application:** China's precision approach to fertilizer management offers crucial strategies for Caribbean nations struggling with high fertilizer costs and environmental concerns about runoff into fragile marine ecosystems.

## üåç **International Cooperation and FAO's Strategic Role**

The **Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO)** demonstrated its catalytic role:

**Bridging Knowledge Systems:**
1. **South-South Exchange Platform**
   - Brazil sharing cerrado technologies with African countries
   - China transferring fertilization knowledge to Latin America
   - India sharing adapted drip irrigation systems
   - **Caribbean contributions**: Traditional hurricane-resistant crop varieties

2. **FAO-STB Global Program**
   - 15 countries implementing adapted "Science and Technology Backyard" models
   - Network of 47 participating universities
   - 120 researchers in international mobility

**Relevant Initiatives for Caribbean:**
- **SIDS-Specific Technology Adaptation Fund**: US$ 10 million allocated
- **Caribbean Climate-Resilient Agriculture Program**: Launched in 2025
- **Traditional Knowledge Documentation Initiative**: Recording indigenous agricultural practices

### üáπüáπ **Trinidad and Tobago's Emerging Contributions**

Our delegation presented several homegrown innovations:

1. **Hydroponic Systems for Urban Agriculture**
   - Developed by University of the West Indies (UWI) researchers
   - Using local materials for reduced costs
   - Perfect for limited urban spaces in Port of Spain

2. **Cocoa Agroforestry Models**
   - Integrating cocoa production with timber and fruit trees
   - Increasing farmer income by 40% through diversified production
   - Enhancing climate resilience through mixed cropping

3. **Solar-Powered Irrigation for Small Islands**
   - Pilot project in Tobago showing 60% reduction in energy costs
   - Community-managed systems increasing water use efficiency
   - Scalable model for other Caribbean islands

## üáøüá¶ **African Development and New Partnerships**

African delegations brought promising perspectives:

**Success Cases:**
1. **Kenya - Solar Micro-Irrigation Systems**
   - Partnership: University of Nairobi + local companies + FAO
   - Cost: US$ 500 per irrigated hectare
   - Impact: Tripled vegetable production in semi-arid areas

2. **Ghana - Post-Harvest Storage**
   - Hermetic technologies developed with Brazilian support
   - Grain loss reduction: from 25% to 8%
   - 50,000 small-scale producers benefited

3. **Ethiopia - Resilient Seeds**
   - Development of drought-tolerant varieties
   - Partnership: Ethiopian Research Institute + CIMMYT + Embrapa
   - 15 new varieties released in the last 3 years

**Lessons for Trinidad and Tobago:** African experiences in drought management and post-harvest technologies offer direct relevance to our challenges with water scarcity and food preservation in tropical humidity.

**Specific Caribbean Initiatives Presented:**

1. **UWI-CARICOM-Industry Consortium**
   - **Students**: Regional graduate exchange (50+ students/year)
   - **Professors**: Joint thesis supervision, co-publications
   - **Enterprises**: Technology adaptation for Caribbean conditions

2. **Caribbean "Student-Entrepreneur" Program**
   - Universities providing space for agricultural startups
   - Companies mentoring and investing in promising ideas
   - **Result**: 15 agri-tech startups created, 5 already with market-ready products

3. **Shared Laboratories Initiative**
   - University infrastructure used by agribusinesses for R&D
   - Companies funding applied research scholarships
   - **Win-win model**: Cost reduction for companies, resources for universities

**Collaboration Data:**
- **Joint scientific publications**: 200% increase since 2022
- **Co-owned patents registered**: 34 in the last 5 years
- **Jobs created** in spin-off enterprises: 850 positions
- **Economic return**: US$ 3.80 in innovation for every US$ 1 invested
